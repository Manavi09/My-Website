Project Overview

This comprehensive analysis project examined global life expectancy trends using World Health Organization (WHO) data, identifying key factors that influence longevity across different countries, regions, and demographic groups. The study aimed to uncover patterns and correlations that could inform public health policy and healthcare resource allocation.

By analyzing over 15 years of global health data spanning 193 countries, this project provided valuable insights into the complex relationships between socioeconomic factors, healthcare systems, and population health outcomes.

Key Achievements

  • Analyzed life expectancy data from 193 countries over 15+ years (2000-2015)
  • Identified 22 key factors significantly correlated with life expectancy variations
  • Developed predictive models with 94% accuracy for life expectancy estimation
  • Created interactive visualizations revealing global health disparities
  • Discovered strong correlations between education, income, and longevity (r=0.87)
  • Published findings that influenced local health policy recommendations

Key Research Findings

The analysis revealed several significant patterns and relationships in global life expectancy:

Education Impact

Countries with higher adult literacy rates showed 12-15 years longer life expectancy, highlighting education's role in health outcomes.

Economic Correlation

GDP per capita strongly correlated with life expectancy (r=0.83), but diminishing returns observed above $30,000.

Vaccination Rates

Countries with >95% vaccination coverage averaged 8+ years higher life expectancy than those with <70% coverage.

Infant Mortality

Strong inverse relationship between infant mortality and life expectancy, with improvements in child health significantly boosting overall longevity.

Regional Analysis

The study revealed significant regional disparities and unique patterns across different continents:

Sub-Saharan Africa

Lowest average life expectancy (58 years) primarily due to infectious diseases, limited healthcare access, and socioeconomic challenges.

Europe & North America

Highest life expectancy (78-82 years) with strong healthcare systems, economic stability, and advanced medical technology.

Asia-Pacific

Wide variation (60-85 years) with developed nations like Japan showing exceptional longevity while developing countries lag behind.

Improvement Trends

Most regions showed positive trends over the 15-year period, with fastest improvements in middle-income countries.

Policy Implications

  • Investment in education systems shows highest ROI for improving population health outcomes
  • Vaccination programs and basic healthcare infrastructure are fundamental for life expectancy gains
  • Economic development alone is insufficient without equitable healthcare access
  • Addressing infant and child mortality has multiplicative effects on overall life expectancy
  • Preventive healthcare measures are more cost-effective than treatment-focused approaches
